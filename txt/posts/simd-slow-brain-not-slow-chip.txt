  SPECIAL -- A LADDER FROM FOUR NUMBERS TO ONE MATRIX CELL
  Most Slow SIMD Is a Slow Mental Model, Not a Slow Chip
  ============================================================================================


  Hand a person four facts -- S is single, I is instruction, M is multiple, D is data -- plus a
  matrix and a pile of intrinsics, and watch what gets built. Measured below, on one ordinary
  laptop: a matrix multiply written by a memorizer runs 0.469 seconds. A plain loop with no
  intrinsics at all runs 0.062 seconds. Memorized SIMD came out SEVEN times SLOWER than writing
  no SIMD whatsoever. Every number identical, every answer correct. Only speed collapsed.

  Silicon did not fail there. A mental model did. Almost every slow or wrong SIMD program traces
  back to a picture in a head, not a flaw in a chip. So a picture is what gets built here, one
  rung at a time, from four bare numbers up to one cell of a matrix product -- numbers and arrows
  first, C code second, a timed race last.

  Two questions ride along at every rung, and answering them out loud before reading on is how a
  picture sticks instead of a quotation:
      ?   happy path  -- what must happen for a right answer?
      ??  unhappy path -- what quietly breaks it, or makes it crawl?

  --------
  FOUR NUMBERS RIDE IN ONE ADD, NO ARRAY REQUIRED

      lane:   0   1   2   3
              1   2   3   4
          +  10  20  30  40
          =  11  22  33  44          one add instruction, four results at once

  No array yet. Four numbers sit side by side inside one 128-bit slot; four more sit in a second
  slot; one add produces four answers together. A whole word of SIMD, drained of mystery:
  four numbers, one instruction, four results.

      ?   happy: four answers appear together -> 11 22 33 44.
      ??  unhappy: a beginner waits for ONE answer, then hunts for where a "sum" went. No sum was
          asked for. Four independent results is correct.

  --------
  WHICH MEANS AN ARRAY IS ONLY NEIGHBORS PULLED IN FOUR AT A TIME

      array:  1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8
      grab 1 2 3 4 -> double -> 2 4 6 8
      grab 5 6 7 8 -> double -> 10 12 14 16
      result: 2 4 6 8 10 12 14 16

  Eight numbers, covered by two grabs of four neighbors. Neighbors, because four values glued
  together in memory ride into one slot in a single pull.

      ??  unhappy, plant a seed now: if four wanted values are NOT neighbors, one pull cannot
          fetch them. Hold a thought there; it decides everything later.

  --------
  A SUM KEEPS FOUR RUNNING TOTALS AND FOLDS ONLY ONCE

      1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8, add it all.

      grab 1 2 3 4
      grab 5 6 7 8
      add slot to slot ->  6  8 10 12      four running totals, one per lane
      fold once        ->  6+8+10+12 = 36

  A sum does not pour eight numbers into one accumulator eight times. Four accumulators grow in
  parallel, one per lane, and collapse into one number a single time, at a very end.

      ?   happy: four partials 6 8 10 12, folded once -> 36.
      ??  unhappy: fold after every grab instead of once, and a cross-lane collapse gets paid
          again and again. Measured later: folding too often runs slower than a plain loop.

  --------
  SWAP PLUS FOR TIMES AND A PRODUCT FALLS OUT UNCHANGED

      1 2 3 4 1 1 1 1, multiply it all.

      grab 1 2 3 4
      grab 1 1 1 1
      multiply slot to slot -> 1 2 3 4
      fold once with x       -> 1*2*3*4 = 24

  Shape did not move. Only an operator changed, plus to times. Four partials, one fold. A sum and
  a product are one skeleton wearing a different operator. Skeleton is a real idea; operator is a
  costume.

  --------
  TWO ARRAYS, ONE ADD, POSITION BY POSITION

      A:  1  2  3  4
      B: 10 20 30 40
      +: 11 22 33 44

  Two feeds, one operator, four results. Lane 0 adds A0 to B0, lane 3 adds A3 to B3. No lane ever
  reaches into a neighbor.

  --------
  AND AN OPERATOR STAYS FREE -- MINUS, TIMES, WHATEVER

      A - B: -9 -18 -27 -36
      A * B: 10  40  90 160

  A feed shape is fixed; an operator floats. Once neighbors ride in a slot, plus or minus or times
  costs one instruction each. Feeding a slot is a real problem; picking an operator is not.

  --------
  A MATRIX IS ONE LONG LINE, PROVEN BY SUMMING IT

      matrix:   1 2 3 4        stored flat as: 1 2 3 4 5 6 7 8
                5 6 7 8

      grab 1 2 3 4
      grab 5 6 7 8
      add -> 6 8 10 12, fold -> 36

  A matrix carries no special power in memory. Row after row, one continuous line. Summing a
  matrix is summing an array, because a matrix already is an array wearing a rectangle.

      ??  unhappy, seed fully grown now: a ROW of a matrix is neighbors -- positions 0,1,2,3. A
          COLUMN of a matrix is NOT -- positions 0, 4, 8, 12, a whole row apart. One pull grabs a
          row for free. A column scatters.

  --------
  SO NOW ONE CELL OF A MATRIX PRODUCT -- C00 AND NOTHING ELSE

  Three entry points, named plainly, before a single multiply:
      code entry     -- first numbers touched: a row of A, a column of B.
      happy customer -- a correct answer C00 must equal a hand result.
      angry customer -- whatever makes a correct answer arrive slowly.

      A row 0:   1  2  3  4
      B:        10 11 12 13      <- row 0
                20 21 22 23      <- row 1
                30 31 32 33      <- row 2
                40 41 42 43      <- row 3

  One cell is one row of A walked across, times one column of B walked down:

      C00 = 1x10 + 2x20 + 3x30 + 4x40
          =   10 +  40 +  90 + 160
          = 300

      ?   happy: a dot product, walking DOWN column 0 of B: 10 20 30 40, lands 300.

  --------
  WHICH TEMPTS A MEMORIZER INTO A DOT PRODUCT INSIDE A SLOT

  Four multiplies wait; a slot holds four. So grab four from a row of A, four from a column of B,
  multiply, add four into one:

      from A row 0:      1  2  3  4         neighbors, one clean pull
      from B column 0:  10 20 30 40         going DOWN
      multiply:         10 40 90 160
      fold four -> one: 10+40+90+160 = 300

  A correct answer. 300. A memorizer stops here, satisfied, and ships a matrix multiply built out
  of a slot-sized dot product. Two costs hide inside, both invisible to a memorized definition.

      ??  unhappy, cost one: 10 20 30 40 is a COLUMN of B. In memory a column scatters --
          positions 0, 4, 8, 12. No single pull fetches scattered numbers; a chip cracks a "gather"
          into four separate fetches, one per cycle. A wide instruction quietly became four loads.
      ??  unhappy, cost two: four products are four pieces of ONE answer, so a fold into one runs
          at an end of every cell. A collapse per cell, forever.

  Both costs arrive together, out of one decision: a slot was filled with pieces of a single
  answer. Now watch a race pay for one decision.

  ============================================================================================
  SECOND HALF -- ONE LADDER AGAIN, NOW AS RUNNABLE C
  ============================================================================================

  Every rung above, hardcoded and unrolled, four-wide. Compiled with g++ 14.4.0, -mavx2 -O2, on
  an AMD Ryzen 7 5700U. Output printed right under a program, copied from a real run.

```c
#include <immintrin.h>
#include <stdio.h>

int main(void)
{
    /* four numbers, one add */
    float o1[4];
    _mm_storeu_ps(o1, _mm_add_ps(_mm_set_ps(4,3,2,1), _mm_set_ps(40,30,20,10)));
    printf("four numbers:  %g %g %g %g\n", o1[0],o1[1],o1[2],o1[3]);

    /* one array, double each of eight */
    float a[8] = { 1,2,3,4,5,6,7,8 };
    float o2[8];
    _mm_storeu_ps(&o2[0], _mm_mul_ps(_mm_loadu_ps(&a[0]), _mm_set1_ps(2)));
    _mm_storeu_ps(&o2[4], _mm_mul_ps(_mm_loadu_ps(&a[4]), _mm_set1_ps(2)));
    printf("array x2:      %g %g %g %g %g %g %g %g\n",
           o2[0],o2[1],o2[2],o2[3],o2[4],o2[5],o2[6],o2[7]);

    /* sum an array: four partials, fold once */
    float p[4];
    _mm_storeu_ps(p, _mm_add_ps(_mm_loadu_ps(&a[0]), _mm_loadu_ps(&a[4])));
    printf("array sum:     %g (from %g %g %g %g)\n", p[0]+p[1]+p[2]+p[3], p[0],p[1],p[2],p[3]);

    /* product: swap + for * */
    float q[4] = { 1,2,3,4 }, r[4] = { 1,1,1,1 }, w[4];
    _mm_storeu_ps(w, _mm_mul_ps(_mm_loadu_ps(q), _mm_loadu_ps(r)));
    printf("array product: %g\n", w[0]*w[1]*w[2]*w[3]);

    /* two arrays: +, -, * */
    float A[4] = { 1,2,3,4 }, B[4] = { 10,20,30,40 }, s[4];
    _mm_storeu_ps(s, _mm_add_ps(_mm_loadu_ps(A), _mm_loadu_ps(B)));
    printf("two arrays +:  %g %g %g %g\n", s[0],s[1],s[2],s[3]);
    _mm_storeu_ps(s, _mm_sub_ps(_mm_loadu_ps(A), _mm_loadu_ps(B)));
    printf("two arrays -:  %g %g %g %g\n", s[0],s[1],s[2],s[3]);
    _mm_storeu_ps(s, _mm_mul_ps(_mm_loadu_ps(A), _mm_loadu_ps(B)));
    printf("two arrays *:  %g %g %g %g\n", s[0],s[1],s[2],s[3]);
    return 0;
}
```

  Running it prints:

      four numbers:  11 22 33 44
      array x2:      2 4 6 8 10 12 14 16
      array sum:     36 (from 6 8 10 12)
      array product: 24
      two arrays +:  11 22 33 44
      two arrays -:  -9 -18 -27 -36
      two arrays *:  10 40 90 160

  --------
  AND C00 BY A MEMORIZER'S MODEL -- GATHER A COLUMN, REDUCE AT AN END

```c
#include <immintrin.h>
#include <stdio.h>

int main(void)
{
    /* row 0 of A = 1 2 3 4 ; B is 4 wide, column 0 = 10 20 30 40 */
    float A[4] = { 1, 2, 3, 4 };
    float B[16] = { 10,11,12,13,  20,21,22,23,  30,31,32,33,  40,41,42,43 };

    /* four from A row (neighbors) */
    __m128 va = _mm_loadu_ps(&A[0]);
    /* four from B COLUMN 0 -> positions 0,4,8,12 -> scattered -> gather */
    __m128i idx = _mm_setr_epi32(0*4+0, 1*4+0, 2*4+0, 3*4+0);
    __m128 vb = _mm_i32gather_ps(B, idx, 4);

    __m128 prod = _mm_mul_ps(va, vb);             /* 10 40 90 160 -- four pieces of C00 */
    float t[4]; _mm_storeu_ps(t, prod);
    float c00 = t[0] + t[1] + t[2] + t[3];        /* reduce four -> one */
    printf("C00 (gather+reduce) = %g\n", c00);
    return 0;
}
```

  Running it prints:

      C00 (gather+reduce) = 300

  Correct. 300. A gather fetched a scattered column, a reduce folded four pieces into one. Both
  cost real time, and a full matrix pays them per cell.

  ============================================================================================
  LAST HALF -- A RACE, AND A MENTAL MODEL SHOT DEAD
  ============================================================================================

  One 256 by 256 matrix multiply, three ways, each run 20 times, every result checked equal.
  Scalar is a plain triple loop, no intrinsics. My-model is a memorizer's SIMD: per cell, gather
  a column, reduce at an end. Fast broadcasts one value of A across a ROW of B and never reduces.

      check C00: scalar 3045   my-model 3045   fast 3045     (identical answers)
      scalar   time = 0.062 s
      my-model time = 0.469 s      <- SIMD, and SEVEN times slower than no SIMD
      fast     time = 0.031 s      <- SIMD, twice as fast as no SIMD

  Read those two SIMD lines twice. Identical chip. Identical answer. One SIMD program runs 15
  times slower than another SIMD program, and 7 times slower than a beginner loop never once
  touching an intrinsic. A processor did nothing wrong. A picture did.

  --------
  WHY A MEMORIZER'S SIMD CRAWLS -- GATHER PLUS REDUCE, PER CELL

  Cost one, a gather. A column of B scatters across memory, so a wide pull cracks into four
  separate element fetches. Cost two, a reduce. Four products are pieces of one cell, so a
  cross-lane fold runs at an end of every cell. Multiply both costs by 256x256 cells, and a
  slow picture buries fast silicon.

  A memorized definition -- single instruction, multiple data -- predicts none of it. It cannot,
  because both costs live in memory layout and loop shape, not in a definition. A poser recites S,
  I, M, D and walks straight into a gather. Days vanish chasing a compiler flag, blaming a chip,
  reading a manual, when a whole fault sits in one sentence: a slot was loaded with pieces of one
  answer.

  --------
  ONE CHANGE FIXES BOTH -- HOLD FOUR ANSWERS, NOT FOUR PIECES OF ONE

  Stop putting four pieces of C00 into a slot. Put four DIFFERENT answers into it: C00, C01, C02,
  C03, one per lane. To feed four different answers, take ONE value of A, copy it four times, and
  multiply across a ROW of B, because C00 C01 C02 C03 differ only by column, and columns 0,1,2,3
  of one row are neighbors:

      A[0][0] = 1  ->  copy 4x  ->  1  1  1  1
      B row 0 (neighbors):          10 11 12 13
      multiply:                     10 11 12 13
      drop into four boxes:         C00=10  C01=11  C02=12  C03=13

      next value of A row, next ROW of B, add on top:
      A[0][1]=2 x B row 1: 20 21 22 23  ->  boxes 50 53 56 59
      A[0][2]=3 x B row 2: 30 31 32 33  ->  boxes 140 146 152 158
      A[0][3]=4 x B row 3: 40 41 42 43  ->  boxes 300 310 320 330

  Box 0 finished at 300 -- identical to a hand result. Boxes 1, 2, 3 arrived at 310, 320, 330 for
  free, in one walk. Watch both costs die from one decision:

      four pieces of ONE answer      ->    four DIFFERENT answers
      must fold boxes (reduce)       ->    never fold boxes           = no reduce
      feed DOWN a column (scatter)   ->    feed ACROSS a row (neighbor) = no gather

  A gather and a reduce were never two separate bugs. Two shadows, one choice: which direction a
  slot slices. Slice down a dot product -> pieces of one answer, fed from a scattered column.
  Slice across outputs -> different answers, fed from a neighbor row. Flip a slice, both costs
  flip, in one stroke. A fast column of a race above did exactly so, and clocked 0.031 seconds.

  --------
  WHICH A MEMORIZER CANNOT FAKE -- FLIP STORAGE AND WATCH A WINNER SWAP

  A real picture survives a twist a quotation cannot. Store B by COLUMN instead of by row. Now a
  column of B is neighbors, and a row scatters. Which model wins?

      ?   happy recall: grab whatever direction sits glued together in memory.
      ??  unhappy recall: never pull across a scattered direction.

  A memorizer's dot-product model, slow a moment ago, becomes fast, because a column it wanted is
  now contiguous. A broadcast-row model, fast a moment ago, becomes slow. Neither model is holy.
  A only rule holds: grab a contiguous direction, refuse a scattered one. A person carrying a
  picture answers in a second. A person carrying four letters -- S I M D -- cannot answer at all.

  --------
  SO A CLOSING FACT FOR ANY BRAIN FROZEN HERE

  A memorized definition builds a matrix multiply 7 times slower than a beginner loop, and 15
  times slower than a good one, on identical silicon. A gap is a mental model, start to end.

  A struggle here carries famous company. A top high-performance course spends a whole week on
  ordering these loops. An author of a world's linear-algebra library co-wrote a paper on doing
  exactly a cell above. A standard math library ships a kernel hand-written in assembly for every
  chip, because no compiler earns it alone. If a ladder above bent your brain at a column, a
  hardest working part of a subject is precisely where a brain bent.

  Memorize four letters and produce slow code. Carry a picture -- neighbors ride in a slot, a
  column scatters, hold answers not pieces -- and produce fast code on a matrix never seen before.
  A difference was never a chip.
