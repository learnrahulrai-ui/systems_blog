  Plain notes on how systems actually behave when you push them: sockets, epoll, the TCP
  window, mmap, the kernel's non-paged pool. Each note starts from a program that broke,
  reproduces the break, and does not stop until the fix is earned. No hand-waving past the
  hard part, no frameworks, no JavaScript -- just the machine, watched closely.

  Every finding here comes from a real run: the byte counts, the crashes, the error numbers
  are copied from the terminal, not guessed.

  [Why a Brand-New Process Inherits Another's Network Speed](posts/inherited-network-speed.txt)
    2026-07-15 . special . tcp, kernel, rfc-2140
    Five fresh processes connect to five different loopback IPs, and the second one starts
    fast for no reason it can see. The kernel, not the code, set the speed -- it cached the
    congestion window against the whole subnet route (RFC 2140) and handed the warmed-up
    metrics to an unrelated process. The real byte counts, the 212,992-byte gears, and why
    blocking sockets leave you at the mercy of state you never wrote.
